The Alphabet Symphony

Letters, sounds, and your first reading technique

1.1

What Is the Alphabet?

The English alphabet is a set of 26 symbols called letters. Each letter represents one or more sounds in spoken English. This relationship — the idea that a written mark corresponds to a spoken sound — is called the alphabetic principle, and it is the single most important concept in all of reading.

Think of the alphabet as the keys on a piano. No single key makes music on its own, but together, in the right combinations and sequences, they produce every song that has ever been written. Letters work the same way. Individually, they are just shapes. Combined according to the patterns of English, they produce every word, every sentence, every story.

1.2 — Critical Distinction

Names vs. Sounds

Critical Distinction: The letter "B" is named "bee." But in the word "bat," it does not say "bee" — it says its SOUND: /b/. Throughout this book, sounds are written between slashes like this: /b/, /s/, /m/.

Every letter has two things: a name (what you call it when you recite the alphabet) and a sound (what it says inside a word). These are different, and confusing them is one of the most common early mistakes.

1.3

Uppercase and Lowercase

Every letter has two forms: uppercase (also called capital letters) and lowercase (also called small letters). They represent the exact same sounds. The choice between them is a matter of grammar rules — not a difference in sound.

1.4

Controlled Blending: Your First Technique

The method used throughout this book is called Controlled Blending. Here is exactly how it works:

1 Look at the first letter only. Say its sound aloud.
2 Look at the second letter only. Say its sound aloud.
3 Look at the third letter only. Say its sound aloud.
4 Blend all three sounds together smoothly.
